## Description
As the food system's emissions are much greater in the growing phase than in the transportation phase, we propose that countries retain freedom of food trade. Restrictions on food trade will generally increase growing emissions by more than they will decrease transportation emissions.

## Background
Crippa et al. (2021) [^"14"] estimates the world agriculture system to be responsible for 34% of greenhouse gas emissions. Food trade is highly globalized, with most staple commodities moving across borders. Bulk shipments are largely transported by sea, which is relatively low in greenhouse gas intensity around 4 grams carbon dioxide-equivalent per tonne-kilometer for bulk carriers and 8 gCO₂e/t-km for cargo ships [^"12"]. This allows regions with favorable climates, soils, and resource conditions to supply others that are less suitable for certain crops. Research consistently shows that the production phase dominates greenhouse gas emissions: farm activities, fertilizer use, livestock methane, and land-use change typically account for about 85% of food’s footprint, whereas transport contributes on average about 6% [^"4"]. Air-freighted perishables are the main exception.

About 23% of the food produced for human consumption is traded internationally [^"1"]. Many regions of the world are in conditions of food shortage due to a local imbalance between production and demand [^"1"].

Freer trade enables sourcing from regions where production is most efficient and least carbon-intensive (e.g., field-grown vegetables from warm climates versus heated greenhouse production in colder ones); the World Bank estimates that this effect reduced world emissions by 390 to 954 million tons per year, or 0.9% to 2.2% of the world total [^"11"]. By importing rather than forcing domestic production of high-footprint foods, countries avoid the need for intensive irrigation, heavy fertilizer use, or deforestation to create new croplands. Trade liberalization typically consolidates flows into bulk shipping routes, which are more energy-efficient than fragmented short-haul or greenhouse-heavy local systems.

Freer trade reduces vulnerability to local climate shocks, pests, and crop failures by diversifying supply. Farmers in efficient producing countries gain market access, thus lifting incomes and spreading agricultural innovations. Countries facing water scarcity can import water-intensive crops instead of depleting their own freshwater supplies [^"6"]. Broader trade increases access to fresh fruits, vegetables, and plant proteins year-round, contributing to greater variety and overall improvements in health.

The energy spent in car use for eating out has been estimated at 20 megajoules per outside meal [^"2"]. It is estimated that the environmental impacts of car based shopping are greater than those of transport within the distribution system itself [^"2"].

## Calculations
Each year, 14 billion metric tons of food are transported globally [^"13"].

## Implementation

The conclusion that trade reduces emissions is complicated by the fact that trade might increase overall emissions by shifting diets toward high-emissions alternatives. Li et al. (2023) [^"7"] found that from 2000 to 2019, emissions embodied in imported food increased by about 1 billion tons of carbon dioxide-equivalent, or about 1% of total world greenhouse gas emissions. They perform a structural decomposition analysis and find that population growth and per-capita demand increases accounted for nearly half of this increase, while improving carbon intensity of food offset some of the increase. The authors furthermore find that from 2000 to 2015, as a result of emissions-intensive crops, especially beef and oil crops, trade increased overall emissions, while trade has decreased overall emissions since 2015.

Such leakage underscores the importance of directly addressing agricultural emissions with a border adjustment, something that is not currently done with the European Union's carbon border adjustment mechanism [^"8"]. In addition to border adjustments, restrictions on imports of products associated with deforestation are an option to mitigate these negative effects [^"9"]. Zech and Schneider (2019) [^"10"] find that under a European Union carbon tax, without a border adjustment, 43% of the expected emissions savings are lost to leakage.

There will need to be an agreement between nations that will be monitored by the United Nations. This may be done with a gradual lowering of import tariffs and quotas on food goods, especially where emissions data show clear environmental benefits from trade. Alignment of sanitary and phytosanitary measures internationally by adopting [Codex Alimentarius]( https://www.fao.org/fao-who-codexalimentarius/en/) guidelines can reduce "hidden" trade barriers. Also important are global standards for measuring and labeling product-level emissions so that trade flows reflect environmental efficiency and embedding environmental safeguards in trade deals to avoid leakage (e.g., preventing deforestation-driven exports) [^"6"].

While freer food trade, in most circumstances, would reduce greenhouse gas emissions in food production, it is widely agreed that the [impact of dietary choice](https://www.urbancruiseship.org/matter/diet/diet_fw), especially reducing animal based consumption, leads to a much larger reduction [^"3"], [^"4"], [^"5"].
