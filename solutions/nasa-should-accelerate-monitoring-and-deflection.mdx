## Background

While many potentially dangerous impactors have been found, there are still civilization-threatening near-Earth asteroids that have not been discovered, and there are many more dangerous asteroids. Furthermore, several dangerous comets have been discovered. NASA's efforts and budget for planetary protection should be increased.

NASA defines a potentially hazardous asteroid as an asteroid that is at least 140 meters in size and with an orbit within 7.5 million kilometers of Earth's orbit around the sun [^"9"]. NASA recently approved the Near-Earth Object Surveyor, a satellite space telescope that will use a heat-sensing infrared telescope, for a launch date of 2027 [^"12"]. NASA's Double Asteroid Redirection Test (DART) is the first asteroid deflection attempt and successfully altered the trajectory of an asteroid [^"11"].

Our assessed benefits of an expanded asteroid detection and deflection program are based in the reduction of catastrophic risks. They included the avoidance of direct physical effects, including immediate damage caused by the asteroid impact, such as air blasts, thermal radiation, cratering, seismic shocks, and tsunamis, which can result in medical harm and infrastructure damage [^"4"]. Indirect social benefits include avoidance of the risk of economic disruption, political reactions, and potential cultural impacts, which would be extensive after a major impact [^"4"]. A large impact would also cause significant environmental damage, such as reducing sunlight reaching the Earth's surface, damaging the ozone layer, and causing global firestorms. Preventing such impacts can avert potential global catastrophes [^"4"]. Finally, enhanced detection and deflection would reduce the risk of an accidental nuclear war that would be caused by an asteroid explosing being misinterpreted as a hostile attack [^"4"].

The current program is spending $150 million annually for planetary defense in the United States [^"7"]. A $250 million annual budget would allow for a program that would provide survey redundancy and support for a space mission for deflection [^"10"].

## Calculations

We can use other common natural disasters to attempt to assess the scale of potential damage that could be caused by an asteroid [^"14"]. A large asteroid strike capable of killing 1.5 billion people has an estimated probability of 1 in 250,000 per year [^"15"]. We estimated that the budgetary increase would reduce this risk by half. Assuming an average lifespan of 72 years [^"16"], or 36 remaining years per person killed, and an average global gross domestic product per capita of $20,693 [^"17"], the annualized benefit of impact risk reduction is $2.2 billion.

We estimate costs as the full cost of the expanded program, including the $150 million per year that is already spent.

The impact of smaller asteroids is also substantial. In 2013, an 18 meter meteor over Chelyabinsk Oblast in Russia exploded in an air burst, injuring 1,491 people and causing $33 million in economic losses [^"13"]. The Chelyabinsk meteor falls far below the threshold of a 140 meter asteroid, and its trajectory for impact was at a glancing angle, reducing the potential damage [^"18"].

We acknowledge great uncertainty in the calculations. In particular, our estimate of the risk of a catastrophic asteroid impact, and thus the benefit from reducing that risk, may be greatly underestimated. The risk is based on the current loss of wealth and life, and it does not consider the loss that will occur many years and decades after the impact.

## Implementation

NASA's Planetary Defense Budget has grown from $4 million a year in 2005 to 2010 to $150 million a year in 2020[^"7"]. Congress can approve a further increase in this budget to assist the program in meeting its goals.
