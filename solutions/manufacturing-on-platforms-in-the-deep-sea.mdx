## Description

We propose appropriating used oil rigs, retired ships, and other stationary platforms for industrial manufacturing to reduce air pollution damage to humans and the biodiverse environments.

## Background

If the demand for oil drops as alternatives gain market share, there may be a surplus of offshore oil rigs. These rigs could be appropriated for industrial or other uses [^"1"],[^"2"],[^"3"]. Retired ships, such as the ocean linear Queen Elizabeth 2, can also be repurposed for more stationary uses [^"4"]. There are several potential uses for converted offshore oil and gas rigs.

### Green Hydrogen

The use of converted rigs for green hydrogen production, powered by offshore wind power, is under active development [^"11"]. Offshore green hydrogen production has the advantage of being able to utilize stronger, steadier offshore wind [^"12"]. Offshore green hydrogen, as a self-powered facility, avoids the need for a grid interconnection, which strains the grid in an environment of grid congestion [^"13"]. However, at present, high offshore wind costs and harshness of seawater toward electrolyzer equipment cause offshore green hydrogen to cost an estimated $4-6 per kilogram of hydrogen, compared to $2-3 for hydrogen from onshore sources [^"14"].

### Carbon Capture

Depleted oil well make for good carbon storage sites [^"15"], but not all offshore well are geologically suitable [^"16"]. The Porthos project in the Netherlands, scheduled to begin operation in 2027, is an example of the use of carbon capture and sequestration in the North Sea [^"17"].

### Habitats

Abandoned oil rigs make for ideal reef habitats. In the United States, the Rigs-to-Reefs program was established after Congress passed the National Fishing Enhancement Act in 1984, and as of 2016, 515 platforms had been converted into reefs [^"18"].

## Implementation

Jurisdictions typically require that oil rigs be decomissioned after production has ceased, which may preclude viable alternative uses for the rigs several years in the future. One fix would be to suspend the decomissioning clock for those projects that have credible alternative uses under development [^"19"].

Part of the initial investment cost could be paid for through grants, similar to the grants awarded by the Gulf States Marine Fisheries Commission and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration to turn an offshore platform in the Gulf Coast of the U.S. into a fish farm [^"7"].
