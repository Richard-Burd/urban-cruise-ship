## Description
We recommend further research and development into electrolysis for steel production.

## Background

World decarbonization requires low-carbon methods of steel production. One method for doing so, hydrogen reduction, is discussed elsewhere. Humbert et al. (2024) [^"6"], assess direct hydrogen reduction and three electrolysis methods: aqueous hydroxide electrolysis, molten salt electrolysis, and molten oxide electrolysis.

Molten oxide electrolysis (MOE) for steel production provides an advancement in the efforts to decarbonize one of the most energy-intensive and emitting sectors. MOE entails heating iron ore to temperatures of around 1600 °C using electricity in a liquid solution. This separates the iron ore into iron and oxygen, akin to the Hall-Héroult process of making aluminum from bauxite, but at much higher temperatures [^"1"].
Iron ore is directly put through an electrolysis furnace to produce liquid steel. This contrasts with the multi-step process of DRI that involves pelletizing iron ore, reducing it in a furnace, and then processing the iron in an electric arc furnace to make steel [^"1"].
MOE can use iron ore of any grade, unlike DRI which typically requires higher grade ore [^"1"].
The primary advantage of MOE is its potential to completely decarbonize steel production. By using low-carbon electricity, the MOE process can convert crude iron ore directly into high-purity molten iron without CO2 emissions [^"2"].
MOE technology is modular and can be scaled in smaller increments [^"2"].

## Calculations

Since there is no steel produced today by electrolysis on an industrial scale, it is difficult to estimate how the cost of such steel would compare with conventional blast furnace-basic oxygen furnace steel. Humbert et al. (2024) [^"6"] estimate that the per-ton levelized cost, taking into account capital expenditures and operating costs, would exceed that of BF-BOF steel but be less than hydrogen direct reduction of iron steel, as described in our green steel analysis. Boston Metal, the leading company that is working to develop MOE, claims that the process will match BF-BOF on price [^"2"], but this claim cannot be independently verified.

## Implementation
MOE requires a continuous and stable supply of electricity, which could be a challenge in areas with unreliable power grids. The total electricity requirement for MOE is around 4 megawatt-hours per tonne of steel, slightly higher than the estimated 3.2 MWh for hydrogen-based DRI production. This technology would need to be coupled with low-carbon energy production [^"3"].

MOE must still overcome significant technical challenges, including the durability of the anode and slag management [^"7"].
