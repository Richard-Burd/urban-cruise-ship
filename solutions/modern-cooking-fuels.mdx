## Background

The International Energy Agency estimates that 2.3 billion people lacked access to modern cooking fuels as of 2022 [^"1"]. Under the current trajectory, that number is projected to reduce to 1.8 billion by 2030, and while progress toward universal clean cooking fuels is continuing, the rate of progress is slowing [^"1"]. Modern fuels include natural gas, liquified petroleum gas, electricity, and renewables such as biodiesel and bio-ethanol. These fuels can be used more efficiently with improved cookstoves [^"4"].

The benefits of improved cooking include social and economic development--including productivity and the savings of time that would be spent gathering wood and fuel--improved health, gender impacts, and environmental benefits [^"4"]. There are also satisfaction benefits such as reduction in smoke, speed of cooking, ease of use, and taste of the food [^"5"]. Indoor air pollution from traditional biomass is one of the leading causes of preventable death in low-income countries, implicated in 2.9 million premature deaths in 2021 [^"6"].

## Calculations

We estimate the cost of an expanded clean cookstove program from the International Energy Agency's "Access for All" scenario, which envisions universal access to clean cooking tools by 2030, based on 2022 data [^"1"]. The IEA estimates that needed investment to achieve universal access is $8 billion per year, of which $2.5 billion per year is already invested. The gap is $5.5 billion per year over eight years, which we annualize at a 7% discount rate.

We estimate the benefit from the Transition Scenario 2--the middle scenario of three presented--of Das et al. (2025) [^"2"]. We note that there are other estimates, such as that of Energy Sector Management Assistance Program (ESMAP) [^"3"]. The ESMAP benefit estimate is a much more aggressive $2.4 trillion per year, though it may use too high of an estimate of the value of a statistical life, among other contested modeling decisions.

## Implementation

The International Energy Agency is well-positioned to take leadership on expanding clean cooking access. In 2026, the IEA formally integrated the Clean Cooking Alliance, which had previously mobilized $2.2 billion of funding [^"8"]. From 2015 to 2022, the World Bank mobilized $562 million of funding for clean cooking [^"9"]. In 2024, the African Development Bank committed $2 billion for clean cooking [^"10"]. Each of these funds, as well as spending by national governments of countries lacking access and donors in wealthy countries can expand spending.

The International Energy Agency [^"1"] notes that an expansion of clean cooking will require a 3% increase in modern energy. Particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, expanded energy distribution will require expanded infrastructure. Sambodo et al. (2025) [^"7"] observe that, among G20 support for clean cooking, 50-70% of spending is directed toward energy subsidies, with a large portion in the form of liquified petroleum gas. They argue that this approach is inefficient and causes fossil fuel lock-in and suggest that one-off subsidies for electrification of cooking and induction stoves is a more effective use of funds.

Investments need to be based on sound research, and the magnitude of the effects of cleaner cooking is in dispute. For example, Checkley et al. (2021) [^"11"] found little health benefit from switching traditional cookstoves to liquified petroleum gas, perhaps because the study period was not long enough. Another challenge is fuel stacking, whereby households who receive a modern cookstove also retain the older, traditional stove [^"12"].
