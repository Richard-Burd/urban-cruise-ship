## Description

We propose that the U. S. Department of Agriculture develop low-cost polyculture techniques and disseminate them through education and outreach.

## Background

Monoculture is the practice of cultivating a single crop over a wide area and poses significant risks to agriculture, including increased vulnerability to pests and diseases, soil degradation, and reduced biodiversity. Monoculture crops are more susceptible to pests and diseases, leading to higher pesticide use and potential widespread crop failure [^"1"]. Continuous cultivation of a single crop can deplete soil nutrients, reduce soil fertility, and increase erosion [^"1"]. Monoculture practices diminish agrobiodiversity, impacting ecosystem services like pollination and natural pest control [^"3"]. A study in Malaysia has found an increase of greenhouse gas emissions related to the widespread monoculture crops for palm oil [^"4"].

Agroecology integrates ecological principles into farming practices, promoting biodiversity, efficient water use, soil health, and effective pest control. This approach includes diversifying crops, implementing crop rotations, and using natural pest control methods [^"5"].

## Implementation

Governments can provide financial incentives for farmers to adopt agroecological practices. This could include subsidies for organic farming, payments for ecosystem services, or tax breaks for sustainable agricultural practices [^"6"].

Increasing funding for agroecological research and incorporating agroecology into agricultural education curricula would help in developing new agroecological techniques and training the next generation of farmers [^"6"]. Policies such as providing extension services that offer advice on crop rotation, soil health, and natural pest control method would help farmers transition to agroecology [^"7"].

Developing and enforcing regulations that limit the use of harmful pesticides and fertilizers, and promote soil and water conservation practices, would also help the transition [^"6"].
